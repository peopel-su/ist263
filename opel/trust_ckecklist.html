Revocable Trust – Trustee Checklist

A practical, printable list to help you work through your responsibilities as trustee after your dad’s passing. Use this as a working copy and bring it to meetings with attorneys or advisors.

Personal Reference Only Prepared for: Trustee
Tip: Click “Print Checklist” or use Ctrl+P / Cmd+P.
Initial Steps
Start here, in the first days and weeks.
  • Obtain certified copies of the death certificate
    Request several copies (often 5–10) from the funeral home or vital records office.
  • Locate the trust and estate documents
    Find the original revocable trust, any amendments, your dad’s will (if any), and any written instructions.
  • Gather key financial documents
    Recent account statements, life insurance policies, retirement statements, loan documents, deeds, vehicle titles, and business records.
Understand the Trust & Notify People
Clarify your role and keep everyone informed.
  • Read the trust document carefully
    Review who the beneficiaries are, what they are to receive, and any special instructions or timelines.
  • Confirm your authority as trustee
    Make sure you are the current trustee (and note any co‑trustees or successor trustees).
  • Notify beneficiaries
    Inform all beneficiaries named in the trust of your dad’s passing and your role as trustee. Some states require written notice.
  • Notify key institutions
    Contact banks, investment firms, life insurance companies, and employers, providing death certificates and trust documentation as requested.
Identify & Protect Trust Assets
Create a clear inventory and secure everything.
  • Create a full inventory of trust assets
    List all assets held in the trust: bank and investment accounts, real estate, vehicles, business interests, and significant personal property.
  • Confirm which assets are titled in the trust
    Check account registrations and deeds to distinguish trust assets from assets that may still be in your dad’s individual name.
  • Secure physical property
    Lock and insure the home, safeguard valuables, and update mailing addresses so you receive important mail.
  • Maintain insurance coverage
    Ensure homeowners, auto, and liability policies remain in force while the trust is being administered.
Debts & Ongoing Expenses
Handle bills carefully and document everything.
  • Identify outstanding debts and obligations
    Review mail and statements for credit cards, loans, medical bills, utilities, and subscriptions.
  • Understand creditor notification rules
    Ask an attorney whether your state requires formal notice to creditors or publication in a newspaper.
  • Pay appropriate expenses from trust/estate funds
    Funeral costs, last medical bills, utilities, property taxes, insurance, and other necessary expenses related to trust assets.
Tax Responsibilities
Coordinate with a tax professional if possible.
  • File your dad’s final income tax return
    Covers the period from January 1 through the date of death. A CPA can help you gather what’s needed.
  • Determine if trust or estate tax returns are required
    Some trusts must file their own income tax returns (Form 1041 in the U.S.) after death.
  • Pay any taxes due
    Use trust or estate funds to pay federal, state, and local taxes before making final distributions.
Distribute Assets to Beneficiaries
Follow the trust document exactly.
  • Confirm that debts, taxes, and key expenses are handled
    Before distributing assets, ensure there are sufficient funds to cover all obligations.
  • Follow the trust’s distribution instructions
    Make distributions exactly as described (specific gifts, percentages, or continuing trusts for certain beneficiaries).
  • Transfer or retitle assets
    Work with banks, brokerages, and the county recorder (for real estate) to move assets into beneficiaries’ names.
  • Obtain written receipts from beneficiaries
    Have beneficiaries acknowledge what they received and the date, for your records.
Recordkeeping & Professional Help
Protect yourself by documenting your work.
  • Keep detailed financial records
    Track all income, expenses, and distributions. Save statements, checks, and receipts in one organized place.
  • Provide accountings if required
    Some trusts or state laws require periodic accountings to beneficiaries. An attorney can help you prepare these.
  • Consult an estate attorney
    Especially if there are disputes, complex assets, property in multiple states, or unclear instructions.
  • Work with a CPA or tax professional
    To handle final and fiduciary tax returns, and to avoid missing important deadlines.
This checklist is a general reference and not legal or tax advice. Laws vary by state and situation. Discuss your specific circumstances with a qualified attorney or tax professional.
